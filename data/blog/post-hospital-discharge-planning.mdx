The period immediately following a hospital discharge is one of the highest-risk windows in an older adult's health journey. Studies consistently show that a significant portion of older adults are readmitted to the hospital within 30 days of discharge — many due to preventable complications that occur at home.

A well-coordinated transition to home care can close these gaps and dramatically reduce readmission risk. Here is what Maryland families need to know.

## What Is Discharge Planning?

Discharge planning is the process — ideally begun on the day of admission — of preparing a patient to leave the hospital safely. A hospital social worker or care coordinator typically leads this process, working with the medical team to assess what level of support the patient will need after discharge.

For older adults, this assessment should address:

- Mobility and fall risk
- Medication management
- Wound care or post-surgical needs
- Follow-up appointments
- Cognitive and emotional state
- The home environment itself

Unfortunately, discharge planning is often rushed. Families sometimes receive a discharge notice with less than 24 hours' notice and insufficient guidance about what comes next.

## Questions to Ask Before Your Loved One Leaves the Hospital

The single most important thing a family can do is prepare a list of questions to ask the discharge team. Do not assume everything has been arranged. Ask directly:

- What medications are being prescribed, and how do they differ from what my parent was taking before?
- Are there dietary restrictions or activity limitations?
- What are the warning signs that something is wrong, and who should we call?
- Has a follow-up appointment been scheduled, and with whom?
- Is home health (skilled nursing or therapy) being ordered, or is this a private-pay home care situation?
- What equipment will be needed at home, and has it been ordered?

If you are not satisfied with the answers, you have the right to request a patient advocate or ask to speak with the attending physician directly.

## Understanding the Difference: Home Health vs. Home Care

Many families confuse Medicare-covered home health services with private-duty home care. They serve different purposes:

**Medicare Home Health** provides skilled nursing, physical therapy, and occupational therapy to homebound patients. It is short-term and medically focused. It does not cover companionship, personal care, or daily household assistance.

**Private-Duty Home Care** (what MarkoCare provides) offers assistance with daily living activities — bathing, dressing, mobility, meals, medication reminders — for as many hours per day as needed. It does not require a physician's order and is not time-limited.

Most post-hospital patients need both, and the two services work together. The home health nurse addresses clinical needs; the home care aide ensures safety and support through the full day.

## Preparing the Home Before Discharge

Where possible, use the time before your loved one returns home to reduce hazards and set up the environment for recovery:

- Remove loose rugs and clutter from pathways
- Install grab bars in the bathroom (or confirm they are already in place)
- Place frequently used items within easy reach
- Ensure the bedroom, bathroom, and kitchen are all on the same floor if mobility is limited
- Stock the refrigerator with appropriate foods

## The Role of Home Care in Reducing Readmission

A trained home care aide observing a patient daily is one of the most effective safeguards against readmission. Aides who are properly supervised by a registered nurse are trained to recognize and report changes — increased swelling, confusion, shortness of breath, wound changes — before they escalate into emergencies.

## Starting the Conversation Early

If you know a family member will be hospitalized for a scheduled procedure, reach out to a home care agency before admission. Pre-arranging care means that aides can be in place by discharge day — not days later when the most vulnerable window has already passed.

If the hospitalization is unexpected, contact a home care agency as soon as you have a discharge timeline. Reputable agencies can often arrange care within 24 to 48 hours.
