The physician visit is one of the most underutilized opportunities in an older adult's care journey. Appointments are short, older adults often downplay their difficulties in a medical setting, and family members who may have critical observations are sometimes excluded from the conversation.

If you have concerns about a parent or loved one's ability to manage safely at home, learning how to raise those concerns effectively with their doctor can open doors to important support.

## Why Families Often Stay Silent

There are several common reasons family members hold back:

- **Not wanting to go behind their loved one's back.** Many adult children feel uncomfortable sharing concerns without their parent's knowledge or permission.
- **Uncertainty about what to say.** Vague worries can feel hard to articulate in a clinical setting.
- **Fear of triggering a chain of events.** Families sometimes worry that raising concerns will lead to hospitalization, forced placement, or conflict with their parent.
- **Deference to the physician.** It can feel presumptuous to tell a doctor what to do.

All of these concerns are understandable. But the cost of staying silent can be your loved one's safety.

## Before the Appointment: Document What You Have Observed

The most effective medical advocates come prepared with specific, objective observations. In the weeks before an appointment, keep a simple log:

- Dates and descriptions of any falls or near-falls
- Specific medication errors you have witnessed or discovered
- Examples of confusion, disorientation, or memory lapses
- Changes in weight, appetite, or personal hygiene
- Any safety incidents in the home

Specific, documented observations carry far more weight with a physician than general impressions like "I'm worried she isn't managing well."

## Communicating With the Physician

If your loved one is comfortable with you being present at the appointment, attend. Many older adults actually want family support during medical visits — the reluctance is sometimes the family member's own.

If your loved one prefers privacy, you can still communicate with the physician separately. HIPAA does not prevent you from sharing information with a doctor — it limits what the doctor can share with you. Call the office before the appointment, explain that you have observations you would like the doctor to be aware of, and ask the best way to convey them.

During the appointment, be direct and specific:

- "I want to share some things I've observed at home that I'm worried about."
- "She has fallen three times in the past two months. Here is what I noticed each time."
- "I found a week's worth of medications untouched in the pill organizer."

Frame your concerns around safety and function, not personality or judgment.

## Questions to Ask

Once you have shared your observations, ask directly:

- **"Does this rise to the level of concern you would act on?"** This forces the physician to take a position rather than being reassuring without follow-through.
- **"What do you recommend to address this at home?"**
- **"Would a referral to a geriatrician, neurologist, or occupational therapist be helpful?"**
- **"Would home health or home care services be appropriate at this point?"**
- **"What are the warning signs I should watch for, and at what point should I bring them to your attention?"**

## Following Up

The most common failure point in primary care for older adults is follow-through. Recommendations made in one appointment are not always acted on before the next visit.

After the appointment, confirm in writing:
- What the physician's assessment was
- What referrals were ordered
- What changes were made to medications or care instructions
- What the plan is going forward

If a referral was made and weeks pass without contact from the specialist, follow up with the physician's office. Being a persistent, organized advocate for your loved one is not being difficult — it is being necessary.

## When the Doctor Is Not Enough

Physicians can identify problems and recommend services, but they cannot coordinate everything that needs to happen at home. A home care agency, a geriatric care manager, or the local Area Agency on Aging can help bridge the gap between what the doctor recommends and what actually happens each day in your loved one's home.
